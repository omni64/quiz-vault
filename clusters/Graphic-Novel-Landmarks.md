# Graphic novel landmarks

Almost no signal for this sub-subject in the Jeopardy archive mined for this vault — the canon here comes from general quizzing knowledge instead, so treat it as a starting point to expand, not an exhaustive list.

- **Alan Moore & Dave Gibbons's *Watchmen*** (1986–87) — deconstructed the superhero genre; the only graphic novel to appear on *Time*'s "100 Best Novels" list.
- **Art Spiegelman's *Maus*** — depicts the Holocaust with Jews as mice and Nazis as cats; won a **special Pulitzer Prize** in 1992, the only graphic novel to do so.
- **Marjane Satrapi's *Persepolis*** — autobiographical account of growing up in Iran during and after the Islamic Revolution.
- **Neil Gaiman's *The Sandman*** — DC/Vertigo series centred on Dream (Morpheus), one of the seven **Endless**.
- **Alan Moore & David Lloyd's *V for Vendetta*** — dystopian, gave rise to the now-widely-used **Guy Fawkes mask** as a real-world protest symbol.

> [!tip] Hook
> "Only graphic novel to win/appear on [prestigious literary list]" is the standard way this sub-genre gets tested — **Watchmen** (Time 100) and **Maus** (Pulitzer) are the two answers to have ready.

## Cluster
- **Frank Miller's** ***The Dark Knight Returns*** (1986) reinvented Batman as older, brutal, and paranoid — hugely influential on later film adaptations.
- **Chris Ware's** ***Jimmy Corrigan: The Smartest Kid on Earth*** is a landmark of literary/art-house graphic novel design.
- The term "**graphic novel**" was popularised by Will Eisner with *A Contract with God* (1978), often cited as an early example of the form.
