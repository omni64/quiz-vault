# World currency chestnuts

The core WQC ask here is blunt: **name the currency of country X** (or vice versa). Own the odd ones out first — they're what separates a guess from a known answer.

- **Botswana's Pula** literally means **"rain"** (also "blessing") — a rain-scarce economy naming its money after the thing it needs most. The classic "what does this currency's name mean" chestnut.
- **CFA Franc** — used by **14** African countries in two separate unions (West African CFA franc, 8 countries incl. Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire; Central African CFA franc, 6 countries incl. Cameroon, Gabon), both pegged to the **euro**, both still guaranteed by **France**.
- **Eastern Caribbean Dollar** — shared by **8** small island states/territories (incl. Antigua & Barbuda, Dominica, Grenada, St Lucia) via the Eastern Caribbean Currency Union.
- **Pound sterling** is the **oldest currency still in use**, tracing to Anglo-Saxon England (~8th century).
- **Kuwaiti Dinar** is conventionally cited as the **highest-valued currency unit** in the world.

> [!tip] Hook
> WQC loves "match the currency to the country," especially where the name itself is the clue (Pula = rain; Vatu, Vanuatu; Ngultrum, Bhutan).

## Cluster
- **Ngultrum** (Bhutan) is pegged 1:1 to the **Indian Rupee**, which also circulates alongside it.
- **Kwacha** — used by both **Zambia and Malawi**; the word means "dawn."
- **Dinar** is legal tender in a wide, easily-confused spread of countries: Kuwait, Bahrain, Jordan, Iraq, Libya, Algeria, Tunisia, Serbia.
- Countries that officially adopted the **US Dollar** with no currency of their own: **Ecuador, El Salvador, Panama, East Timor**, plus Pacific states **Marshall Islands, Micronesia, Palau**.
- **Switzerland's franc** also circulates in **Liechtenstein**.

## Mnemonic
"Pula = rain, Vatu = Vanuatu, Ngultrum pegged to the rupee" — the name-tells-the-story currencies are the ones worth over-learning; everything else is memorisation.
