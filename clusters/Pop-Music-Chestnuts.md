# Pop music — genre-defining tracks

- **Nirvana's "Smells Like Teen Spirit"** (1991) is the canonical song credited with breaking **grunge** into the mainstream.
- **Fleetwood Mac's *Rumours*** (1977) — written amid the band's internal breakups — includes "Go Your Own Way" and "Dreams," and remains one of the best-selling albums ever.
- **Queen's "Bohemian Rhapsody"** (1975) famously has **no chorus**, structured instead as a mini rock-opera (ballad, guitar solo, opera section, hard rock, outro).
- **Michael Jackson's *Thriller*** (1982) is the **best-selling album of all time**.
- **"We Are the World"** (1985) — charity single for African famine relief, written by Michael Jackson and Lionel Richie, produced by Quincy Jones.

> [!tip] Hook
> Debut-album and best-seller superlatives recur constantly: know the **"first," "best-selling," "breakthrough"** answer for each major act, not just the song titles.

## Cluster
- The **British Invasion** (mid-1960s) — led by **The Beatles**' 1964 arrival in the US — brought UK acts (Rolling Stones, The Who, Kinks) to American chart dominance.
- **Disco** peaked commercially with **Saturday Night Fever** (1977, Bee Gees soundtrack) before a sharp backlash ("Disco Demolition Night," 1979).
- **Whitney Houston's "I Will Always Love You"** (1992, *The Bodyguard*) was originally written and recorded by **Dolly Parton**.
- **KC and the Sunshine Band** are the answer behind several 1970s one-word-title disco/funk hits ("That's the Way (I Like It)," "Get Down Tonight").

## Mnemonic
Best-seller anchors: **Thriller** (album), **"Smells Like Teen Spirit"** (genre-breaker), **Rumours** (breakup album) — three different superlatives, three different answers.
